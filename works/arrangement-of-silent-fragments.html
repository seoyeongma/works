Arrangement of Silent Fragments

2026

Clay pigeon

440 x 182 x 1.5 cm

The floor installation reconstructs the floor plan of the Natural History Museum Vienna using fragments of clay pigeons designed to shatter upon impact from gunfire. Rather than treating these fragments as mere residue, the work approaches them as unstable materials that resist fixation, employing fragmentation both as a material condition and as a methodological approach.

By reassembling a material intended to be destroyed and discarded into the architectural structure of a museum, the work generates a tension between two opposing systems: destruction and preservation. While clay pigeons are consumable objects that fulfill their function only through breakage, the museum, by contrast, seeks to collect, classify, and preserve traces of life over time. The work does not simply put these systems side by side, but reveals their underlying interdependence.

While the reconstructed floor plan follows the symmetry and order of the museum’s architecture, it is composed of irregular and fragile fragments. Rather than forming a unified whole, these pieces introduce instability into a structure that typically represents permanence and authority. In this context, the fragment operates beyond its material condition, pointing to scientific classification and exhibition practices as constructed and mutable systems.

The work reveals how acts of conservation have historically been closely entangled with processes of extraction and destruction. By reconstructing an institutional space through materials marked by traces of violence, the installation questions the presumed neutrality of the museum and reframes preservation as a process shaped by selection, transformation, and ongoing change.
